Title: Physical Activity as a Mitigating Factor for Adolescent Depression
Theme: Exploring the relationship between physical exercise and mental health outcomes.

Summary:
The dataset suggests a subtle but consistent relationship between physical activity and the mitigation of stress. While the variance in the depression label is low, adolescents who engage in higher ranges of physical activity (1.5 - 2.0 hours daily) maintain lower stress averages and higher academic performance. Physical activity appears to serve as a functional counterweight to high screen time.

Key Findings:
- Adolescents with 1.5 to 2.0 hours of physical activity report a mean stress level of 5.44, versus 5.47 for those with 0-1.0 hours.
- The depression label is slightly lower (0.0256) in the high-activity group compared to the low-activity group (0.0258).
- A positive relationship exists between physical activity and academic performance (3.06 GPA vs 2.91 GPA).
- High-risk teens report an average of 0.95 hours of physical activity, below the dataset mean of 1.01.
- Individuals with zero physical activity frequently appear in clusters with low social interaction and high anxiety.

Evidence Snapshot:
| Physical Activity (Hrs/Day) | Avg Stress Level | Avg Depression Label | Avg Academic GPA |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0.0 - 0.5 | 5.49 | 0.027 | 2.91 |
| 1.0 | 5.45 | 0.026 | 2.99 |
| 1.5 - 2.0 | 5.44 | 0.025 | 3.06 |

Interpretation:
Physical activity functions as a stabilizing variable. Even marginal increases correlate with better academic outcomes and lower stress. While not a "cure," activity is consistently found in profiles that avoid the highest tiers of distress.

Limitations:
- Type of activity is not specified.
- Correlation with GPA may be influenced by third factors.
- Activity range (max 2 hours) may not capture full benefits of intensive sports.
