Title: Social Interaction Levels and the Paradox of Social Anxiety
Theme: Evaluating how levels of social interaction correlate with stress and anxiety.

Summary:
A counterintuitive trend emerges: high levels of social interaction are associated with higher average stress and anxiety scores. This "socially active distress" suggests that for many teens, the pressure of maintaining high engagement may contribute to psychological tension.

Key Findings:
- Teens with "high" interaction levels have the highest average anxiety (5.70) and stress (5.51).
- "Low" interaction levels correlate with the lowest average anxiety (5.52) and stress (5.35).
- "Medium" interaction serves as a middle ground (Anxiety: 5.68, Stress: 5.48).
- High social interaction does not significantly lower addiction levels.
- The depression label is stable across interaction levels, suggesting social volume is a poor predictor compared to sleep.

Evidence Snapshot:
| Social Interaction Level | Avg Anxiety Level | Avg Stress Level | Avg Addiction Level |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| High | 5.70 | 5.51 | 5.54 |
| Medium | 5.68 | 5.48 | 5.57 |
| Low | 5.52 | 5.35 | 5.58 |

Interpretation:
The data challenges the assumption that more social interaction is always beneficial. For adolescents, "high" interaction may imply a constant state of being "on," leading to elevated stress.

Limitations:
- Does not distinguish between online and face-to-face interaction.
- Interaction levels are self-defined qualitative metrics.
- Personality types are not controlled for.
